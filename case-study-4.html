Context

The Problem

VR platforms were being positioned as the future of distributed work — but almost no evidence existed on whether they could support real knowledge work tasks, not just social presence.

"Most VR platforms were designed for social experiences. The question was whether they could support actual work."

Research Goals

What I Set Out to Learn

  • 🧠 Understand how knowledge workers protect productivity and focus in distributed environments
  • 🥽 Evaluate whether VR supports deep-focus individual work
  • 🤝 Examine how VR changes collaboration dynamics vs. video meetings
  • 📐 Define the design requirements for VR workspaces that support real work

Approach

Two Studies in Sequence

First establishing a baseline understanding of productivity outside VR, then evaluating VR's effect on those same behaviours.

Study 1 — Baseline

Remote Work Productivity

~50 knowledge workers across 4 countries. 90-min contextual interviews, work observations, and collaboration workflow journey mapping.

Study 2 — VR Evaluation

Productivity in VR

~25 VR users + observed live sessions. 16+ hours of recorded collaboration, semi-structured interviews, and framework analysis.

Analysis methodology
Framework analysis and journey mapping
▼

A framework analysis was combined with journey mapping across both studies. Framework analysis was chosen for its ability to systematically compare thematic material across a large multi-source dataset — interview transcripts, observational notes, and session recordings.

Journey mapping modelled moment-to-moment transitions between individual deep work and collaborative activities, capturing where remote tools introduced friction and where VR offered something different.

Analysis process: Inductively coded from the data, not a predefined framework. A second researcher independently coded a subset for reliability. Findings triangulated against existing workplace design and organisational psychology research.

Findings

Four Key Insights

  • 🧘
    VR creates a psychological boundary that supports focus. The headset's physical barrier was the most consistently cited benefit — participants entered focused states more easily in VR than any 2D remote setup.
  • 🤝
    VR meetings work — but only when content interaction is possible. Participants felt "in the same room" and reported more equal participation, but only when they could annotate and interact with shared content. Passive VR meetings rated no better than video calls.
  • 🔔
    Notification overload is the primary productivity barrier — in VR and outside it. Most participants had built manual workarounds (muting apps, scheduling focus blocks) — the tools weren't solving the problem.
  • 🔋
    Hardware is the ceiling on adoption. Despite strong intent, headset discomfort and interaction limitations — note-taking, task-switching — were universally cited as barriers to sustained daily use.

Output

VR Productivity Framework

Four dimensions required for VR environments to genuinely support productive work.

🏗️
Environmental Design
Dedicated focus and collaboration spaces with fluid switching between them. Customisable environments that reflect personal and team identity.
💪
Physical Comfort
Ergonomics, lighter hardware, and longer battery life as prerequisites for daily adoption — not optional improvements.
🧩
Cognitive Support
Context-aware interruption management, availability signalling, and tools that protect focus states rather than adding to notification load.
🔗
Behavioural Support
Active content interaction during meetings, task prioritisation tools, and communication that doesn't overwhelm.
